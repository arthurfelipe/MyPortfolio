Is cloud computing actually worth it?

By Arthur Felipe
July 12, 2021

SUMMARY: In the IT field, there are many strong opinions for and against cloud computing. But what is the fuss all about? Is it actually worth it? Are some better than others?

If you open a YouTube video that even touches the subject of cloud computing, you will find people on the comments section typing either A) Cloud is the worst thing ever created or B) Cloud is magical. Reality check: It isn't either. Why?

In the past, cloud resources were a complicated type of business, since subscription-based plans only gained credibility in the last couple of years (mainly because of streaming services like Netflix, Hulu, Spotify and many others). The fear of being overcharged or having your information leaked was very strong, and it was justified.

DATA BREACHING AND OVERCHARGING

There are 2 main ways where your data could be exposed when working with cloud computing: Your sensitive data being leaked (like credit card info, personal or company data, and so on) because of weak digital security practices, or, content regarding your project being leaked (like databases and such), since you are trusting your sensitive data to another company. As time went on, new security measurements were implemented (look around, we don't even mind looking up the site' security certificates we are buying things from). This, in pair with the fact that most of the cloud resources are generally offered by well-known and well-trusted tech companies (like Google, Microsoft and Amazon) keeps bringing more assurance to people their data is actually secure (from malicious users, because these companies WILL use your data how and whenever they want to, as described in their TOSs). On top of that, these platforms also offer you, the developer, a bunch of counter measurements against DDoS & OWASP that, in the end, if well set up, should back you up against plenty of attacks (cloud-specific or even general web attacks, what is a very solid argument for these services). Platforms like Google's GCP also feature load-balancing, security policies, real time monitoring and many other features, just like your traditional server setup, usually with no extra charges.

Overcharging is also a big concern when we talk about cloud computing. We have all heard companies that made one silly mistake which cost them upwards of 20 THOUSAND dollars. And you also have normal, everyday users that slipped their fingers and sent 1 thousand bucks down the drain in a couple of hours. The most common counter measurement is to set a spend limit (which, as far as I know Google offers and insists that you use it in their course). You can also set usage limits, so you don't exceed your quota. On top of that, some companies don't want to be the "bad guys": Google doesn't want you to spend 5 grand by mistake, keep it and then risk their own image or stock prices. That may vary a lot, since you will be relying solely on the company' mercy and customer relationship policy. My tip for you? Don't rely on that. Keep a very tight spending quota, so if things go south, you and your company are both safe.

EASE OF USE AND RESOURCES

Working with either locally hosted servers or cloud hosted servers requires some knowledge, especially if you are, for example, dealing with an important website, known to have many visitors around the world, and should have >99% of availability. My biggest experience is with Google's GCP, and as far as I can tell, the UI is very straight-forward, documentation is VERY well done and there are plenty of courses! Google even offers you plenty of hands-on experience with their courses, which I absolutely recommend (and this post is not sponsored by any means, I just dig the service a bunch). I've had more problems with AWS, but that's out of the scope.

PRICING

That's the fun part. If you have big needs, to host a local server, you have to consider:

  • Buying the server itself;
  • Having a team for maintenance, update and set up;
  • Buying add-ons (like room cooling solutions, cables, routers, switches, no-breaks, sound deadening liners...);
  • Dealing with disasters (like fire, water, dust, animals, intruders...);
  • Dealing with downtime for maintenance or updates;
  • Electrical expenses;
  • Enterprise-level internet connection expenses;
  • A separate room only for the server...

    • And the costs only go up. I have no doubts that the expense, plus the inconvenience of hosting your own server is enough to make you move to a cloud hosted service. Don't get me wrong, it is still going to be expensive, but less. And, usually, you pay for what you use, so in a month that you use less resources, you will actually pay less than usual.

    For small businesses, I also recommend cloud-based alternatives, since there are very low-buck options that are more than capable of dealing with the load, and the AVAILABILITY and RELIABILITY are just unbeatable (for the price, at least). Way better than using a 7y.o. dual core as a makeshift server.

    But, if you have a medium-sized business that can get by with a core i7 or i9, I really recommend sticking to the locally hosted server (if you can deal with downtime in some way). Why? In this tier, cloud hosting prices are actually quite high, to the point that, in the long run, it's cheaper to have your own solution. On top of that, a core i7 or i9 server is still quite small and isn't much of a hassle. Of course, you will have to come up with your own solution regarding attacks and updates, but that's on you to decide. Also, you have to consider your company's growth: Does your company have the chance of, in the near future, becoming a big company, with big needs? If yes, maybe you should consider a cloud-based solution from the very beginning, so you don't have to spend money twice (buying a decent local server and then after a couple of years having to rent a web server is no good). That's why it is so important to know your business and plan every spend with caution. Don't rely on a guy on the internet, real data is unbeatable.
